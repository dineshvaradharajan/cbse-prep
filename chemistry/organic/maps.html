The 11 functional groups you must recognise instantly. Each group has its own characteristic reactions — master these structures first.

🌀 Aromatic Foundation

Benzene Ring Foundation
C₆H₆
Foundation of aromatic chemistry. All positions equivalent. EAS reactions preferred (not EAd).

Ch 10 — Haloalkanes & Haloarenes

Alkyl HalideCh 10
R–X
X = F, Cl, Br, or I. Named haloalkane (aliphatic) or haloarene (aromatic).
Aryl HalideCh 10
Ar–X
Haloarenes are less reactive to nucleophilic substitution due to C–X bond resonance with ring.

Ch 11 — Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

AlcoholCh 11
R–OH
–OH (hydroxyl) group. Primary, secondary, tertiary based on C bearing the OH.
PhenolCh 11
Ar–OH
More acidic than alcohol (phenoxide ion stabilised by resonance with ring).
EtherCh 11
R–O–R'
Ethers are relatively inert — good solvents. Cleavage occurs only with conc. HI or HBr.

Ch 12 — Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

AldehydeCh 12
R–CHO
Carbonyl carbon (C=O) at end of chain. Always has H on carbonyl C.
KetoneCh 12
R–CO–R'
Carbonyl C flanked by two carbons. Cannot be oxidised further without breaking C–C bonds.
Carboxylic AcidCh 12
R–COOH
Most acidic common organic group. Carboxylate anion (RCOO⁻) is resonance-stabilised.
EsterCh 12
R–COO–R'
Formed from carboxylic acid + alcohol. Pleasant fruity smell. Hydrolysis gives back acid + alcohol.
AmideCh 12
R–CONH₂
Weakest acid among carbonyl derivatives. C–N bond has partial double-bond character (resonance).

Ch 13 — Amines

Amine (Primary)Ch 13
R–NH₂
Basic character due to lone pair on N. 1° > 2° > 3° basicity in gas phase, reversed in water.

⚗️ Carbonyl Chemistry Hub

The C=O group is the star of organic chemistry. Everything attacks the electrophilic carbon. Learn who attacks and what you get.

C=O (Carbonyl Group)

The carbon is electron-poor (δ+) → nucleophiles attack it
Cδ+=Oδ−
Aldehyde (R−CHO) is MORE reactive than Ketone (R−CO−R') because:
less steric hindrance + less electron donation to C=O
🔑 Key Principle
Nucleophilic Addition = nucleophile (Nu⁻) attacks the electrophilic C
→ π bond breaks → O becomes O⁻ → protonation → product

Oxidation vs Reduction:
Aldehyde can be oxidised to COOH (Tollens, Fehling — positive for RCHO, NOT for R₂CO)
Aldehyde & Ketone can both be reduced to alcohol (NaBH₄)
🔵
Addition of HCN → Cyanohydrin
Reagent: HCN (base catalyst)
R−CHO + HCN → R−CH(OH)−CN
Mechanism: CN⁻ (nucleophile) attacks C of C=O → tetrahedral intermediate (alkoxide) → protonation by HCN → cyanohydrin
💡 Cyanohydrin contains CN group → acid hydrolysis → α-hydroxy acid (one more carbon than the starting aldehyde)
🟣
Grignard Reagent Addition
Reagent: R'MgX (dry ether)
HCHO → 1° alcohol | RCHO → 2° alcohol | R₂CO → 3° alcohol
RCHO + R'MgX → R−CH(OH)−R' (after H₃O⁺)
💡 Grignard is a carbanion (R⁻ equivalent) — very strong nucleophile. Always write Mg in the formula. Product has one more carbon than starting carbonyl.
🟠
Aldol Condensation
Reagent: dilute NaOH (must have α-H)
Step 1: OH⁻ removes α-H → enolate
Step 2: Enolate attacks C=O of another molecule
Step 3: β-hydroxy aldehyde (aldol product)
Step 4: Heat → dehydration → α,β-unsaturated carbonyl
2 CH₃CHO → CH₃CH(OH)CH₂CHO → CH₃CH=CHCHO
💡 Memory trick: "Aldol = Alcohol + Aldehyde" — product has both groups initially
🔴
Cannizzaro Reaction
Reagent: conc. NaOH (no α-H!)
For aldehydes WITHOUT α-H (HCHO, PhCHO, CCl₃CHO)
One molecule is oxidised → RCOO⁻ (acid)
Other is reduced → RCH₂OH (alcohol)
2 HCHO + NaOH → CH₃OH + HCOONa
💡 Cannizzaro = disproportionation. Key word: "no α-H". Formaldehyde and benzaldehyde are classic examples.
🔷
Reduction — Clemmensen vs Wolff-Kishner
C=O → CH₂ (complete removal)
Clemmensen: Zn(Hg)/conc. HCl (acidic, acid-stable compound)
Wolff-Kishner: NH₂NH₂ then KOH/Δ (basic condition)
R−CO−R' → R−CH₂−R'
💡 Use Clemmensen if product is acid-stable; use Wolff-Kishner if product is base-stable. Both remove the oxygen completely.
🟢
Reactions of Carboxylic Acid
RCOOH → many derivatives
RCOOH + R'OH → Ester (H⁺ catalyst, Fischer esterification)
RCOOH + SOCl₂ → RCOCl (acid chloride — most reactive derivative)
RCOOH + NH₃ → RCONH₂ (amide — then Hoffmann → amine)
RCOOH + LiAlH₄ → RCH₂OH (1° alcohol)
💡 Reactivity of acid derivatives: RCOCl > RCOOH > RCONH₂. Acyl chloride reacts with everything!
🟡
Tollens vs Fehling Test
Distinguish aldehydes from ketones
Tollens (silver mirror test): RCHO + Ag(NH₃)₂⁺ → RCOOH + Ag↓ (silver mirror)
Fehling: RCHO + Cu²⁺(blue) → RCOOH + Cu₂O↓ (brick red ppt)
Ketones: NO reaction with either (no oxidation)
💡 Exception: Fructose (a ketone) gives Fehling's positive because it isomerises to glucose in alkaline conditions
🩷
Acidity of Carboxylic Acids
RCOOH > phenol > alcohol
pKa: RCOOH (~4.8) < phenol (~10) < alcohol (~16)
–I group (Cl, NO₂) near COOH → increases acidity (stabilises RCO₂⁻)
+I group (CH₃) → decreases acidity
ClCH₂COOH > CH₃COOH > (CH₃)₃CCOOH
💡 The closer the electron-withdrawing group to COOH, the stronger the acid. Cl at α-carbon has bigger effect than at γ-carbon.

🔬 Reaction Mechanisms — Step by Step

For full marks, draw curly arrows and name each intermediate. These 4 mechanisms cover ~20 marks in CBSE exam.

SN1 vs SN2 — Nucleophilic Substitution
5 marks

SN1 (Unimolecular)

  • Rate = k[R−X] only
  • 2-step: ionisation → Nu attack
  • Carbocation intermediate
  • 3° substrate preferred (stable C⁺)
  • Racemisation of product
  • Polar protic solvent (H₂O, ROH)
  • Weak nucleophile (H₂O)

SN2 (Bimolecular)

  • Rate = k[R−X][Nu⁻]
  • 1-step: concerted backside attack
  • No intermediate (transition state only)
  • 1° substrate preferred (less steric)
  • Walden inversion (R → S or vice versa)
  • Polar aprotic solvent (DMSO, acetone)
  • Strong nucleophile (CN⁻, OH⁻)
Aldol Condensation — Complete Mechanism
5 marks
1
Deprotonation of α-carbon: OH⁻ removes the α-H (H on carbon next to C=O) → forms enolate ion (carbanion)
CH₃CHO + OH⁻ → ⁻CH₂CHO + H₂O
2
Nucleophilic attack: Enolate carbon attacks the electrophilic C=O of the second aldehyde molecule
⁻CH₂CHO + CH₃CHO → [CH₃CH(O⁻)CH₂CHO]
3
Protonation → Aldol product: The alkoxide O⁻ picks up H⁺ from water → β-hydroxy aldehyde
→ CH₃−CH(OH)−CH₂−CHO (3-hydroxybutanal)
4
Dehydration (on heating) → Condensation product: Loss of H₂O from β-hydroxy group → α,β-unsaturated carbonyl
CH₃−CH(OH)−CH₂−CHO → CH₃−CH=CH−CHO + H₂O (but-2-enal)
Key terms: Aldol product = β-hydroxy aldehyde (step 3 product, cold). Aldol condensation product = α,β-unsaturated carbonyl (step 4, on heating).
Condition for aldol: Compound MUST have α-H. Formaldehyde (HCHO) has no α-H → Cannizzaro instead.
Hoffmann Bromamide Degradation — 4-Step Mechanism
5 marks
1
N-Bromination: Br₂ + NaOH → NaOBr. NaOBr brominates the nitrogen of the amide
RCONH₂ + Br₂/NaOH → RCONHBr (N-bromoamide)
2
Deprotonation: NaOH removes N−H → forms anion (RCON⁻Br)
RCONHBr + NaOH → RCON⁻Br + H₂O
3
Isocyanate formation: Br⁻ leaves → nitrogen migrates with its lone pair → R−N=C=O (isocyanate, KEY intermediate!)
RCON⁻Br → R−N=C=O + Br⁻
This is why the product has ONE LESS CARBON — the C=O of isocyanate is lost next
4
Hydrolysis of isocyanate: H₂O attacks → carbamic acid → decarboxylation → primary amine
R−N=C=O + H₂O → R−NH−COOH → R−NH₂ + CO₂
Memory: "Hoffmann = one less carbon". RCONH₂ (n carbons) → RNH₂ (n−1 carbons).
Example: Ethanamide (CH₃CONH₂) → Methylamine (CH₃NH₂)
Nucleophilic Addition to C=O — General Mechanism
3–5 marks
1
Polarisation of C=O: Oxygen is more electronegative → C is δ+ (electrophilic)
Cδ+= Oδ− ←→ ⁺C−O⁻ (resonance)
2
Nucleophile attacks C: Nu⁻ donates electron pair to C → π bond breaks → O becomes O⁻ (alkoxide)
Nu⁻ + R−CHO → R−CH(Nu)−O⁻ (tetrahedral intermediate)
3
Protonation: O⁻ picks up H⁺ (from solvent or acid) → final product
R−CH(Nu)−O⁻ + H⁺ → R−CH(Nu)−OH
Nu⁻ = CN⁻ → Cyanohydrin
R−CH(OH)−CN → hydrolyse → α-hydroxy acid
Nu⁻ = RMgX → Grignard
→ 1°, 2° or 3° alcohol depending on carbonyl type
Nu = NH₂OH → Oxime
R₂C=O + H₂NOH → R₂C=N−OH + H₂O
Nu = RNH₂ → Imine (Schiff base)
R−CHO + R'NH₂ → R−CH=N−R' + H₂O

🌿 Biomolecules — Visual Quick Maps

7 marks from biomolecules. Focus on: types + linkages + bonds + function. Don't memorise structures — memorise the logic.

🍬 Carbohydrates
MonosaccharidesGlucose, Fructose, Galactose — cannot be hydrolysed further
DisaccharidesSucrose (Glu+Fru), Maltose (Glu+Glu), Lactose (Glu+Gal)
PolysaccharidesStarch, Cellulose, Glycogen
Starch linkageα-1,4 (amylose straight) + α-1,6 (amylopectin branched)
Cellulose linkageβ-1,4 — linear, indigestible. Structural role in plants
Reducing sugarHas free −CHO or open-chain keto: Glucose, Fructose, Maltose, Lactose
Non-reducingSucrose — both anomeric carbons used in glycosidic bond
💡 Test: reducing sugars give Tollens/Fehling positive. Sucrose does NOT — it has no free −CHO or −OH at C1.
🧬 Proteins
MonomerAmino acids (H₂N−CHR−COOH) — 20 types, linked by peptide bonds (−CO−NH−)
1° StructureSequence of amino acids. Bond: peptide (covalent). Determined by DNA.
2° Structureα-helix or β-pleated sheet. Bond: H-bonds (between C=O and N-H of chain)
3° Structure3D folding. Bonds: disulphide (–S–S–), H-bonds, hydrophobic, ionic
4° StructureMultiple subunits. E.g., Haemoglobin (2α + 2β). Non-covalent forces.
DenaturationDisrupts 2°, 3°, 4° (NOT 1°). Heat, acid, organic solvents. Loses activity.
💡 Memory: "1234 = primary(peptide), secondary(H-bond), tertiary(disulphide), quaternary(subunits)"
🧪 Nucleic Acids
MonomerNucleotide = nitrogenous base + pentose sugar + phosphate group
DNA sugarsDeoxyribose (2′-H); RNA: Ribose (2′-OH)
DNA basesAdenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C), Thymine (T)
RNA basesA, G, C, Uracil (U) — no thymine
Watson-CrickA=T (2 H-bonds), G≡C (3 H-bonds). Anti-parallel strands.
DNA vs RNADNA: double-stranded, T instead of U, deoxyribose. RNA: single-stranded, ribose
💡 "AT 2, GC 3" — A pairs with T (2 bonds), G pairs with C (3 bonds). More GC = stronger/harder to denature DNA.
🔑 Enzymes & Vitamins
Enzyme natureProteins (globular). Act as biological catalysts — specific to one reaction.
Active sitePocket on enzyme where substrate binds. Complementary shape to substrate.
Lock & KeyRigid active site matches substrate exactly (like lock and key)
Induced FitActive site changes shape slightly to embrace substrate — more accurate model
Fat-solubleVitamins A, D, E, K — stored in fatty tissues
Water-solubleVitamins B-complex, C — not stored, need regular intake
💡 "Fat-soluble ADEK, water-soluble B&C" — common exam question about deficiency diseases links to which vitamin.

📐 Key Diagrams — Must-Know Visuals

6 high-yield visual concepts that unlock marks across Haloalkanes, Stereochemistry, and Mechanism questions.

SN2 — Nucleophilic Substitution Mechanism Haloalkanes • 5 marks

💡 SN2 only happens with 1° substrates (unhindered). 3° substrates prefer SN1 instead. Backside attack = inversion of configuration = R becomes S or vice versa.

Carbocation Stability Order SN1 • E1 • 5 marks

💡 More alkyl groups = more hyperconjugation + +I inductive effect → disperses positive charge. Benzyl (C₆H₅–CH₂⁺) and allyl (CH₂=CH–CH₂⁺) are also highly stable due to resonance.

Inductive Effect — Electron Push & Pull Acidity • Reactivity

💡 The -I effect of halogens explains: FCH₂COOH > ClCH₂COOH > BrCH₂COOH > CH₃COOH (acidity order). More electronegative = stronger -I = more acidic.

Benzene Ring — Ortho / Meta / Para EAS • Boards favourite

💡 In EAS: –OH, –NH₂, –CH₃ are ortho/para directors. –NO₂, –COOH, –CHO are meta directors. Board questions often ask to predict position of substitution.

Aldol Condensation — Complete Mechanism Carbonyl • 5 marks

💡 Aldol needs α-H (HCHO and (CH₃)₃CCHO have no α-H → Cannizzaro instead). Heating drives dehydration. Crossed aldol = mix of two different aldehydes (less useful, not asked in CBSE boards usually).

Hybridisation — Shapes & Bond Angles Bonding • Geometry

💡 Carbocation (R₃C⁺) is sp² hybridised — it's flat/planar (that's why Nu can attack from both sides → racemisation in SN1). In SN2 the transition state is also sp²-like.
